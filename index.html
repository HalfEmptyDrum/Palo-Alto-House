4080 Amaranta Ave

A rough guide

This is meant as a rough guide for your time at 4080 Amaranta Ave. You probably don't need a guide. You will have a great time without reading all of this. Still, it's a one-minute read. Keep in mind though that it is by no means exhaustive. Either because I haven't done all possible things while I was at the house. But also, because I purposely didn't put everything important in.

I wrote this in one go, so forgive me for bad writing. You will get the point.

01

Start building something.

You are there for a short time, but building stuff is fun and potentially useful. Start sooner than later. Build something you haven't built before.

If possible, try to work with someone else. Your idea is probably good if someone from the house wants to work on it. Also, it is way more fun.

Be willing to build many things — to throw most of them in the bin.

If you actually want to build something useful in these two weeks, talk to people first. No matter how smart you are, you won't come up with ideas any good.

You don't need to build something with the end-goal of it being useful. I would optimize for fun first, usefulness second. Useful things take time, and are potentially boring in the first few weeks.

Don't worry about your idea not sounding cool. Ideas that work are cool. Ideas that sound cool are, well, just ideas.

02

Stop building stuff. Start meeting people.

There are not many places like the Bay. Try to meet as many people as possible. You are only there for a few weeks. The utility of meeting someone new is incredibly high w.r.t. time spent (if you don't already have a deep Silicon Valley network).

Get intros as a start. If you don't know many people and don't know where to start, ask people from the house or other friends who you should meet. Everyone makes intros.

Don't do everything over intros from people in the house. You want to build a unique network. You don't do this by doing everything through intros of your existing intro (you are only walking the same graph everyone else has).

Meet some people more than once.

You can meet some kinds of people without knowing why you want to meet them. But people here appreciate if you have some goal in mind. Also, culture here is by nature transactional. So implicitly show why someone should meet you.

Message people. Don't underestimate how powerful you just being in the Bay is. Most people spoke to me because I was — just there.

Moving around the valley a bit and meeting random people will increase your luck surface tremendously.

You'll meet successful people who seem stupid and unsuccessful people who seem smart. When that surprises you, it means your model of what drives success here is wrong. Think of it as the universe telling you you're wrong. Almost every non-American has a few of these moments. Take the hint.

Stop meeting people. Meeting people feels like you are getting things done (which you can argue you are) without actually accomplishing anything.

03

Contribute to the house.

Organize dinners, prepare topics — whatever you think you can do. The most important thing is that you do something. Doing it extremely well comes second. If you are a great person, you probably contribute by just being there. Feel free to go beyond that though.

04

The Bay is mimetic.

Everyone in the Bay seems like they know what's going on — right jargon, bad clothes, casual boasting. Truth is: almost nobody does. A handful of people actually get it. Use this to your advantage. You're smart enough to read the dynamics quickly. Don't accept everything as status quo. The Bay is probably the most mimetic ecosystem in the world.

  • Visit the Stanford Campus. Best-case: meet Stanford students.
  • Walk to the "Dish".
  • Go for a run through the neighborhood.
  • Go to SF (which you will do anyways by meeting people).
  • Create an X account. Or don't. I am not sure about this one.
